Lime and Bird have been a driving force behind the drastic changes in urban mobility we observe these days. We aim at providing a better understanding of the mechanisms of a scooter operator’s business model.

Based on a mix of real data points and educated guesses, we have built a simulation model that allows you to explore different key components and how strategic decisions may influence each other.

Lime reports that “11% of Parisians report using e-scooters either ‘frequently’ or ‘from time to time’.” You can define an initial user base and explore the effects of different adoption scenarios.

It is not uncommon for cities to charge a fee to operators of dockless vehicles. For instance, in October 2018 the city Louisville in Kentucky, US issued a policy that entailed the following details: “In addition to a license application fee, annual fee, bond and fee for a designation group parking area, operators will pay $1/day per vehicle.” Similarly, The Verge reports “Baltimore charged Bird and Lime each a $15,000 fee up front and a $1 per scooter fee per day. In Raleigh, North Carolina, Bird added a $2 transportation fee on top of its $1 unlock fee after the City Council implemented a $300 per scooter fee.”

By clicking the checkbox, you can decide if or if not to include city fees in any scenario. In the prior case, this implies an annual fee of 50€ per scooter and a daily fee of 1€ per scooter.

In contrast, by allowing to simulate different scenarios you get an idea of how changes in market share may influence different KPIs.

One of the most fundamental business question concerns the number of scooters a provider deploys in the city. More scooters imply greater user comfort as the walking distance to the next scooter decreases. However, it has significant effects on the costs of operation as more scooters need to be purchased, charged, and maintained. Further, the utilization of each scooter decreases. Finally, the question remains: how to adjust the number of scooters once the user base increases?.

In practice, many cities limit the number of scooters any provider is allowed to deploy in order to remain a certain degree of control over negative side effects. For instance, Louisville’s dockless policy specifies: “During the probationary period, operators may begin with 150 vehicles and, if ridership exceeds four rides per vehicle per day, increase their fleet by 100 vehicles per month, up to 650. Ultimately, operators will be permitted 1,050 vehicles if they successfully complete the probationary period and meet performance standards, such as responsiveness to complaints and ridership.” Similar policies can be found in various cities, such as Vienna, Madrid. As of now, there exists no such policy in Berlin.
